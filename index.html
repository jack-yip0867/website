Our Story

Every day, 99% of the world's population breathes air that exceeds the WHO's safety guidelines. In Europe alone, air pollution causes over 400,000 premature deaths each year. Yet the air we breathe is invisible — and so are the patterns of who gets exposed, and why.

This project was born from a simple question: can we see what's invisible? Using free and open satellite data from the Copernicus programme, we set out to map nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) concentrations across the Netherlands — a country where dense urban populations live alongside intensive agriculture and one of Europe's busiest transport hubs.

But pollution doesn't exist in a vacuum. We wanted to understand how land cover changes — urban expansion, deforestation, agricultural conversion — relate to changes in air quality, and ultimately, how these combined factors determine population exposure to health risks.

Over the course of five labs, we processed CAMS atmospheric reanalysis data, ESA WorldCover 10m land cover maps, and WorldPop population counts — each with its own resolution, format, and challenge. We built change detection models, computed bivariate classifications, and created an interactive WebGIS to share what we found.

The result is more than a grade. It's a demonstration that open data and GIS can empower anyone — students, citizens, policymakers — to ask and answer critical questions about the environment we share. In a time of climate change and urban growth, understanding the air we breathe has never been more urgent. And with tools like these, it's never been more accessible.

Project Overview

Analyzing the spatial relationship between air quality, land cover change, and population distribution across the Netherlands using satellite-derived datasets from CAMS, ESA WorldCover, and WorldPop.

Air Quality Analysis

NO2 concentration mapping using CAMS reanalysis data at 0.1 degree resolution (~11km), classified according to EU air quality standards into 5 distinct pollution levels.

Land Cover Change

Built area expansion detection between 2021 and 2023 using ESA WorldCover 10m annual land cover data.

Population Exposure

Population living in different pollution classes assessed using WorldPop counts at ~92m resolution.

Interactive WebGIS

Web-based interactive map built with OpenLayers to visualize and explore all processing results with multiple basemaps, vector layers, and click-to-inspect functionality.

Key Findings

NO₂ & Built Area Analysis

Annual NO₂ ranges from 3.8 to 22.0 μg/m³ — only EU Classes 1 and 2 are present. 76.7% of the population lives in Class 2 areas (10-25 μg/m³). The AMAC analysis shows a mean decline of -2.07 μg/m³ from 2021 to 2023. Built area stability is 95.74%, with new built areas coming mainly from cropland conversion (507: 63.77%).

PM2.5 & Cropland Analysis

PM2.5 decreased across all LCC zones (means: -1.48 to -1.56 μg/m³). Cropland shows a 95.33% stability rate, covering the largest area (2.57 sq.deg). Gain comes mainly from wetland conversion (1105: 41.36%). The main driver of cropland loss is urban expansion (507: 41.52%) and conversion to wetland (511: 34.62%).

PM10 & Tree Cover Analysis

PM10 levels show minimal variation across LCC types (means: -1.49 to -1.65 μg/m³). Tree cover shows a 90.64% stability rate, with new gains from former cropland (502: 56.25%). The main driver of tree loss is urban expansion (207: 32.61%) tied with agricultural encroachment (205: 32.24%).

Overall Land Cover Stability

The Netherlands shows 93.77% overall land cover stability between 2021 and 2023. For built area (Class 7), persistence reaches 95.74%, with the main gain source being cropland conversion (507: 63.77%). All three pollutants — NO₂, PM2.5, and PM10 — declined or remained stable across the study period, with urbanization emerging as the dominant driver of land cover change.

Technologies Used

GDAL / QGIS

Geospatial data processing: reprojection, raster alignment, zonal statistics, and format conversions.

Python

Data analysis, raster processing with NumPy, statistical computations, and visualization with Matplotlib.

OpenLayers

Web-based interactive map with multiple basemaps, vector layers, and GIS controls.

GeoJSON / GPKG

Vector data formats for administrative boundaries and bivariate classification results.
